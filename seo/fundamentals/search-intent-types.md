# Search Intent: The 4 Types and How to Match Them

**Source:** Matt Diggity (Kenyon Digital), Semrush
**Tags:** #seo #search-intent #keyword-research #matt-diggity #semrush

## Core Concept

Matching search intent is the single most important factor in ranking. Your content's format must reflect what Google has already decided searchers want for that query — word count and effort don't matter if the format is wrong.

## The Four Types of Intent

1. **Informational:** The user wants to learn something. (e.g., "how to train your puppy")
2. **Commercial:** The user is comparing options before a purchase. (e.g., "best dog training classes")
3. **Transactional:** The user is ready to buy/act. (e.g., "dog training class near me sign up")
4. **Navigational/Branded:** The user wants a specific website. (e.g., "Petsmart dog training")

## How to Determine Intent

Google your target keyword and study the top-ranking results. The SERP itself is Google's answer to what the user wants. Your job is to match that intent and execute it better than what's already ranking.

## Handling Mixed Search Intent

Some queries (e.g., "logiciel de signature électronique") can serve multiple intents — comparison research vs. direct navigation. Two approaches:

### 1. Adopt the Majority View
Mirror the format of whatever the majority of top results use.

### 2. The "Backdoor" Strategic Approach
If competitors dominate with homepages thanks to massive brand authority, don't try to beat their homepage directly. Instead, create an alternative content format — like a comparison/roundup post — to capture the segment of intent their homepage doesn't directly serve.

## Long-Tail vs. Short-Head Keywords

- **Short keywords** (e.g., "coffee"): high volume, but messy/competitive SERPs and ambiguous intent.
- **Long-tail keywords** (e.g., "best dark roast coffee beans for espresso"): lower volume, but crystal-clear intent and manageable competition.

**Rule of thumb:** Newer sites with less authority should start by building topical authority through long-tail content before attacking short-head keywords.

## Intent in the AI Era

- AI Overviews now appear for commercial and transactional queries, summarizing answers before a user even clicks ("zero-click marketing").
- To earn the click anyway, your content must go deeper than what the AI summary already covers. Surface-level content that just repeats the obvious answer is no longer sufficient — it must add something the AI didn't already say.

## How to Use

1. Identify your target keyword
2. Search it and study the top 5-10 results as a group
3. Note the dominant format (blog post, product page, video, comparison table, etc.)
4. Match that format, then differentiate with depth, experience, or a strategic angle the top results miss
