# Avatar Creation

**Source:** Russell Brunson, Andre Chaperon
**Tags:** #research #avatar #customer-profile #brunson #chaperon

## The Core Principle

Your dream customer is often **you from five years ago.** This makes it easier to remember the pains, questions, and desires they have right now.

## Brunson's Avatar Creation Method

### Step 1: Define the Exact Person
Don't create a demographic profile. Imagine a real, specific person.

**Method:**
- Use Google Images to find a face that matches
- Give them a name and a story
- Describe their current struggles, desires, and daily life

**Brunson's examples:** "Michael" and "Julie" — specific people with specific traits, mindsets, and values.

### Step 2: The "You Five Years Ago" Rule
Ask: "Who was I five years ago?"

- What was I struggling with?
- What questions did I have?
- What did I wish someone would teach me?
- What was I afraid of?
- What did I desire most?

### Step 3: The "Would Serve for Free" Test
Ask: "Who are the people I would serve for free because I love them and the topic?"

If you wouldn't serve them for free, you're building a business you'll eventually hate.

## Chaperon's Identity Model (Frank vs Matt)

Instead of a static avatar, define two identities:

### Frank (The "Before" Identity)
- The struggling version of your customer
- Their pains, frustrations, and failed attempts
- "I'm a Frank"

### Matt (The "After" Identity)
- The successful version they want to become
- What they'll achieve, feel, and be
- "I want to become a Matt"

### Why This Works
The transformation is built into the avatar itself. Your marketing becomes the story of Frank becoming Matt.

## The "Dream 100" Extension (Brunson)

Once you know your avatar, find where they already congregate:
- Podcasts they listen to
- YouTube channels they watch
- Instagram accounts they follow
- Facebook groups they join
- Blogs they read
- Niche websites they visit

Build a list of the top 100 people, platforms, and communities that already have your ideal customers. Go where the fish already are.

## Key Insight

**Define the customer before the product.** The biggest mistake Brunson made early on was creating products first and thinking about the customer later. That led to mismatched buyers and a business he didn't enjoy serving.
