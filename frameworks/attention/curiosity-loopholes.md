# Curiosity Loopholes

**Source:** Sabri Suby, Russell Brunson
**Tags:** #curiosity #attention #hooks #headlines #suby #brunson

## Core Concept

Curiosity is the strongest attention mechanism in marketing. When you create an information gap, the human brain feels tension until it's resolved. This is called an "open loop," and it's the basis of all great hooks.

## The "Loophole Hook" (Suby)

### The Problem with Literal Headlines
A headline like "Learn How to Make Money with Local Reviews" fails because the prospect thinks they already know the answer. They bounce.

### The Fix: The Loophole
Use a curiosity-driven headline with a negative statement:

> "This is the weird loophole we found to help local businesses make extra money. This is not Facebook ads, this is not Instagram... register to find out exactly what this new thing is."

### Why It Works
- Creates an information gap
- Rules out obvious answers (forces curiosity)
- Promises a reveal
- The word "loophole" implies insider knowledge

### Results
Suby reports this fix alone turned a $25 cost-per-lead into a $5 cost-per-lead and doubled show-up rates.

## The Curiosity Mechanism

1. **Open a gap:** Hint at valuable information
2. **Withhold resolution:** Don't give the answer yet
3. **Promise closure:** "Find out exactly what..."
4. **Deliver:** The resolution must be genuinely satisfying

## Examples of Curiosity Hooks

- "The one thing I never told you about..."
- "I said I'd never do this..."
- "What they don't want you to know..."
- "This is embarrassing..."
- "Don't open this email"
- "Can you eat yourself thin?"

## Common Mistake

Giving away the answer in the hook itself. If you resolve the curiosity too early, you lose all momentum. The hook's job is ONLY to earn enough attention for the story to start.
