# Social Proof Stacking

**Source:** Multiple (synthesized from all transcripts)
**Tags:** #trust #social-proof #testimonials #conversion

## Core Concept

Social proof is not a single element—it's a stack of trust signals that accumulate throughout the page, email, or funnel. The strongest proof is specific, verifiable, and placed right before the call to action.

## Types of Social Proof (Ranked by Strength)

### 1. Specific, Measurable Results
**Strongest:** "I doubled my income from $4,200 to $8,400/month in 6 weeks"
**Weak:** "This was great"

### 2. Detailed Case Studies
Show the full journey: problem → implementation → result. Include real numbers, timelines, and specific tactics used.

### 3. Video Testimonials
Higher trust than text because they're harder to fake. Keep them short and results-focused.

### 4. Text Testimonials with Full Name + Photo
Anonymous testimonials carry almost zero weight. Attribution matters.

### 5. Numbers/Counters
"Join 47,000+ subscribers" or "1,035 students enrolled" signals social validation.

### 6. Authority Indicators
- Media logos ("As seen on...")
- Certifications
- Awards
- Industry recognition

### 7. Client/Customer Logos
Especially powerful in B2B. Known brands transfer trust.

### 8. Reviews and Ratings
Aggregate scores (4.9/5 stars) with volume (2,300+ reviews).

## Placement Rules

1. **Above the fold:** At least one trust signal immediately visible
2. **Before CTA:** Strongest proof right before the ask
3. **Throughout:** Sprinkle proof across the page, not all in one section
4. **Pain-specific:** Match proof to the objection it overcomes

## The "Worst Moment" Testimonial Technique (Hormozi)

Don't ask "How was life before?" Ask "What was the WORST moment?"

**Result:** "We were two months away from shutting our doors" instead of "Things weren't going great."

Specific pain makes the transformation more powerful.

## Common Mistakes

- Using fake or exaggerated proof
- Burying proof where no one sees it
- Vague, unverifiable claims
- Proof that doesn't match the prospect's specific objection
